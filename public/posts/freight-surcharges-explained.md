# What Are Freight Surcharges — And Should You Pass Them On to Customers?

If you've ever looked at a carrier invoice and wondered why the total is so much higher than the base rate you expected, surcharges are usually the answer.

Freight surcharges are additional charges applied on top of the base freight rate for specific circumstances — the type of delivery, the nature of the goods, the fuel price on a given week. They're standard across the industry, but they're often not well understood by store owners, which leads to either absorbing costs you shouldn't or surprising customers with charges they didn't expect.

Here's what the main surcharges are, and how to think about passing them on.

---

## The main surcharges you'll encounter

**Fuel levy**
The most universal surcharge. Every major Australian carrier applies a fuel levy — a percentage added to the base freight charge, adjusted regularly (usually monthly or quarterly) based on fuel prices.

Fuel levies typically range from 15% to 30% of the base rate. On a $30 base charge with a 26% fuel levy, you're paying $37.80 before anything else is added. This is significant and should always be factored into your freight calculations.

**Residential delivery surcharge**
Most carriers charge more for deliveries to residential addresses than to businesses. The reason is operational: residential deliveries are more dispersed, less predictable, and often require the driver to attempt re-delivery if no one is home.

Residential surcharges are usually a flat dollar amount per consignment — often $5 to $15 depending on the carrier and zone.

**Tailgate (tail-lift) surcharge**
When a delivery requires the truck's hydraulic tailgate to lower heavy items to ground level — common for furniture, appliances, gym equipment, and pallets — carriers apply a tailgate surcharge.

This is one of the more significant surcharges, often $25 to $50 per consignment. If you're shipping heavy or bulky goods, this will apply regularly.

**Hand unload surcharge**
If a driver is required to manually unload items rather than using a forklift or pallet jack, an additional hand unload charge applies. This typically occurs at residential addresses or premises without loading dock access.

**Overlength / oversize surcharge**
Items that exceed certain dimensions (usually 1.2m or 1.5m in length, depending on the carrier) attract an overlength or oversize surcharge. If you sell long items — timber, pipes, surfboards, furniture with long components — check your carrier's dimension limits carefully.

**Dangerous goods surcharge**
Any items classified as dangerous goods under Australian regulations — certain chemicals, batteries, aerosols, flammable liquids — attract a dangerous goods surcharge. The amount varies significantly based on the classification.

**Saturday delivery**
If a customer requests Saturday delivery, most carriers apply an additional surcharge. This is usually opt-in at checkout rather than automatic.

## How surcharges add up in practice

Here's a real-world example. A furniture retailer ships a 30kg dining table from Melbourne to a residential address in Brisbane.

- Base rate (Zone 5, 30kg): $48.00
- Fuel levy (26%): $12.48
- Residential surcharge: $9.50
- Tailgate surcharge: $35.00
- **Total: $104.98**

The base rate was $48. The actual cost is $104.98. If you quoted $48 flat, you've lost $57 on that order.

This is why flat rate shipping fails for bulky goods stores — the surcharges on individual orders can dwarf the base rate, and a flat rate can't account for that variability.

---

## Should you pass surcharges on to customers?

There's no universal right answer, but here's a framework:

**Pass through at cost** when the surcharge is directly tied to the customer's delivery circumstances — residential surcharge, tailgate, hand unload. These are costs caused by the delivery, not by you. Showing them transparently at checkout is fair and customers generally accept them.

**Build into your pricing** when the surcharge applies to almost every order and is predictable — like the fuel levy. Rather than showing a fuel levy line item at checkout (which looks like a hidden fee), factor it into your freight calculation so the total shown is the total charged.

**Absorb selectively** when the surcharge is small and absorbing it improves conversion more than it costs you. A $5 residential surcharge on a $300 order is probably worth absorbing. A $35 tailgate charge on a $60 order is not.

**Never surprise with surcharges** added after the customer has committed to a freight cost. If a surcharge applies, it should be visible at checkout — not added to the invoice later.

## Surcharges and free shipping

If you offer free shipping over a threshold, decide whether that means free of surcharges too — or just free of the base rate.

**Smart free shipping** waives the base freight charge when the order qualifies, but still applies surcharges like tailgate or residential. This protects you from absorbing large surcharges on bulky orders.

**Always-free shipping** waives everything including surcharges. Generous, but expensive on orders with significant surcharge exposure.

For most stores, smart free shipping is the right default. Reserve always-free for specific promotions or product categories where you've run the maths.

## Getting surcharges right at checkout

The challenge with surcharges in WooCommerce is that most basic shipping plugins don't handle them at all. You either ignore them (and absorb the cost) or apply a blanket handling fee (which overcharges some customers and undercharges others).

The better approach is to load your carrier's actual surcharge schedule and apply charges based on real delivery conditions — residential vs business, tailgate required or not, item dimensions. This gives customers an accurate total at checkout and protects your margin on every order.
