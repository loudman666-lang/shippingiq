# The Hidden Cost of Free Shipping (And How to Offer It Without Losing Money)

Free shipping is one of the most effective conversion tools in ecommerce. Studies consistently show it increases order completion rates, raises average order value, and reduces cart abandonment.

It also costs money. And offered the wrong way, it erodes your margin on every single order.

Here's how to think about free shipping properly — and how to offer it in a way that works for your business, not against it.

---

## Why free shipping works

The psychology is well-documented. Customers don't like paying for shipping. It feels like a tax — money leaving their pocket for something intangible. Even when the total cost (product + shipping) is identical to a competitor's "free shipping" price, shoppers prefer the one where the shipping is zero.

This isn't rational, but it's consistent. Free shipping removes friction at the point of decision. It also makes the total cost feel more predictable — no surprise at checkout.

---

## Why free shipping costs more than you think

The mistake most store owners make is treating free shipping as a marketing expense without calculating what it actually costs per order.

If your average freight cost is $14 and you offer free shipping on everything, you're spending $14 per order on freight — before you've accounted for product cost, packaging, payment processing, or any other overhead. On a $60 order with a 35% gross margin, that's $21 gross profit minus $14 freight — leaving $7 to cover everything else. That's not a business, that's a subsidy programme.

The maths gets worse for bulky goods, regional deliveries, and heavy items. A furniture store offering free shipping to remote Western Australia is often losing money on freight alone.

---

## The threshold approach

The most common — and most effective — way to offer free shipping is with an order value threshold. Free shipping on orders over $150 (or $200, or whatever makes sense for your margins).

This works for three reasons:

**1. It filters out low-margin orders.** Higher order values mean more gross profit to absorb the freight cost. A $200 order with 35% margin gives you $70 gross — much more room to cover a $14 freight cost than a $60 order does.

**2. It increases average order value.** Customers who are $20 away from free shipping will often add another item to qualify. This is well-documented behaviour and it's real — you'll see it in your order data within weeks of switching.

**3. It's transparent.** Customers know the rule, they can work towards it, and there's no surprise at checkout.

---

## Smart free shipping vs always-free

There's an important distinction between two types of free shipping:

**Smart free shipping** waives the freight charge but still applies surcharges in certain circumstances. For example, if a customer qualifies for free shipping but their order includes an oversized item that triggers a tailgate surcharge, that surcharge might still apply.

**Always-free shipping** waives everything — base rate and all surcharges — no matter what.

For most stores, smart free shipping is the right default. Always-free is a generous offer that works well for marketing campaigns or specific product categories, but can be expensive if applied across the board without thinking about surcharge exposure.

---

## Product exclusions

Some products should never qualify for free shipping, regardless of order value. Oversized furniture, items with dangerous goods classifications, or anything that reliably costs $60+ to ship to regional areas.

The cleanest way to handle this in WooCommerce is with a product tag. Tag those items as exempt from free shipping, and your rules engine excludes them automatically — even if the rest of the order qualifies.

This lets you offer free shipping broadly without subsidising the outlier products that would make it uneconomical.

---

## How to set the right threshold

There's no universal answer, but here's a framework:

1. Calculate your average freight cost per order (look at your carrier invoices over the last 3 months)
2. Find your average gross margin percentage
3. Work out the order value at which your gross profit exceeds your average freight cost by a comfortable margin

For example: average freight $16, gross margin 40%. You need an order value of at least $40 just to break even on freight ($40 × 40% = $16). Set your threshold well above that — probably $120–$150 minimum — to leave room for all your other costs.

Then test it. Look at your average order value before and after. If customers are consistently hitting the threshold, you've set it right. If almost no one qualifies, it's too high to drive behaviour change.

---

## The rule of thumb

Free shipping should be funded by the additional margin on higher-value orders — not eaten out of your existing margin on every order. Set the threshold so that customers who qualify are genuinely profitable, and exclude the products that would make it uneconomical.

Done properly, free shipping is one of the best investments you can make in your checkout conversion rate. Done carelessly, it's a slow leak in your margin you might not notice until it's too late.
